01 — The case for story

The biggest stage on Earth is
almost silent on climate.

Entertainment reaches billions of people a day in the exact emotional register where worldviews are formed. Yet the climate crisis barely appears. That silence isn’t a failure — it’s whitespace. For philanthropy, it may be the most undervalued narrative real estate in the world.

0%

of 37,000+ film & TV scripts (2016–20) mention any climate keyword — Good Energy × USC Norman Lear Center

0%

say the words “climate change” out loud — same study

0%

of South Koreans call climate a pressing crisis — while it fills 0.36% of top K-drama screen time — IJoC, 60 dramas 2019–24

0

people play video games — a bigger audience than film and music combined — UNEP Playing for the Planet

“People don’t change their minds because of data. They change their minds when someone they love — even a fictional someone — changes theirs.”

02 — The framework

Work like the tide.Four moves, in any medium.

Blue crabs grow by molting — shedding a shell that no longer fits. So do narratives. The TIDE framework is how we help funders shed the PSA-era playbook and move with culture instead of against it.

T

Tune in

Map the narrative ocean before you make waves. Who already holds the audience’s heart — which shows, artists, leagues, fandoms? Where is climate already present, absent, or distorted?

  • Commission a narrative audit (the 2.8% stat exists because someone funded the counting)
  • Listen to fandoms, not just focus groups
  • Find the whitespace — silence is your map
I

Immerse

Don’t make climate content. Make the content people already love climate-fluent. Embed scientists in writers’ rooms, retrofit the tour, write the storyline — never the lecture.

  • Fund expertise pipelines into creative rooms (the Green Screen model)
  • Back transitional characters, not perfect heroes
  • Let the plot carry the planet — stakes, not statistics
D

Distribute

One story is a ripple; synchronized stories are a tide. Coordinate across rival properties, platforms, and borders — and design every moment for the second screen.

  • Anchor to calendar moments (COP, climate weeks, heat seasons)
  • Give the internet a phrase to carry (“Just Look Up”)
  • Treat fandoms as distribution infrastructure
E

Evaluate

Measure narrative change, not impressions. Did salience move? Did conversation move? Did the policy window move? Publish what you learn so the whole field compounds.

  • Pre/post audience studies, not vanity reach
  • Track “story spillover” into news & social discourse
  • Independent verification beats self-reporting (see: Coldplay × MIT)

03 — The case files

Nine plays that already worked.

Filter by medium. Open a card for the story, the strategic move, the receipts, and the steal-this takeaway you can run in your own portfolio.

TV & Film · US

Grey’s Anatomy: the heat dome comes to Grey Sloan

19 seasons of trust, one deadly heat wave — and not a lecture in sight.

+

The story

For its Season 21 midseason finale, TV’s longest-running primetime medical drama hit Seattle with a deadly heat dome modeled on the real 2021 Pacific Northwest event. Climate arrived not as an “issue episode” but as triage: overheating patients, strained infrastructure, doctors improvising.

The move

Embed expertise, not messaging. Green Screen (co-founded by the CAA Foundation) connected the writers’ room with climate-health experts so the science was load-bearing, never bolted on. The showrunner has said hospitals are so affected by climate that the show will “always continue to revisit” it.

The receipts

Major press coverage, strong audience response, and a recurring climate thread inside one of the most-watched dramas on Earth — institutionalized, not one-off.

Steal this Fund the connective tissue — expert-to-writers’-room pipelines — rather than commissioning “climate shows.” Trust travels through characters audiences already love.
Soaps & Serials · UK

The great British soap crossover, COP26 week

Seven rival soaps. One synchronized climate week. A first in TV history.

+

The story

The week COP26 opened in Glasgow (Nov 2021), EastEnders, Coronation Street, Emmerdale, Hollyoaks, Casualty, Doctors and Holby City all wove climate into their storylines — and crossed characters between rival shows for the first time ever. A Hollyoaks figure turned up in Walford; a Doctors character appeared on the Corrie cobbles.

The move

Synchronize the tide. Months of quiet coordination between competing editorial teams (sparked by Emmerdale’s executive producer, Jane Hudson) turned seven separate ripples into a single national moment, anchored to a policy event.

The receipts

Climate reached tens of millions of British living rooms in a single week, in the country’s most trusted everyday-story format — timed exactly to the global policy spotlight.

Steal this Philanthropy is the natural neutral convener of rivals. Fund the backstage coordination no single broadcaster can lead — and anchor it to a calendar moment like this very climate week.
Soaps & Serials · Global South

Telenovelas & the Sabido method

The 50-year-old proof that serial drama changes behavior at national scale.

+

The story

In 1970s Mexico, producer Miguel Sabido engineered telenovelas around “transitional characters” — flawed people who wrestle their way toward change on screen. Audiences didn’t just watch; they followed. The method drove measurable national shifts in behavior and has since been adapted by organizations like Population Media Center across dozens of countries for health and environmental serials on TV and radio.

The move

Long-arc transformation beats the PSA. Audiences reject perfect heroes and tune out lectures. They lean in when a character they recognize — stubborn, conflicted, theirs — slowly changes.

The receipts

Five decades of evaluated, replicated behavior change across continents — the most evidence-backed entertainment-for-impact methodology in existence.

Steal this When designing any climate storyline, fund the transitional character: the skeptic-becoming-advocate, not the activist who arrived finished. And budget for evaluation from day one — Sabido did.
TV & Film · Global

Don’t Look Up and the second screen

A comet allegory that handed the internet a four-word climate vocabulary.

+

The story

Adam McKay’s satire never says “decarbonize.” It made denial, distraction, and media absurdity the villain — and became one of Netflix’s most-watched films ever, with climate scientists publicly saying it captured exactly how being unheard feels.

The move

Design for the conversation, not just the screen. “Just Look Up” became instant activist shorthand — a meme, a protest sign, a hashtag. The film’s real distribution channel was the argument it started.

The receipts

A record-scale global discourse moment: op-eds, scientist explainers, and weeks of social conversation that mainstream climate coverage rarely achieves.

Steal this Every funded story needs a portable phrase — the line that survives the screen and lives in the feed. Budget for the discourse layer (creators, explainers, partner amplification) before launch, not after.
TV & Film · Korea

K-drama’s climate whitespace — and the fans filling it

90% of Koreans call climate a crisis. Their dramas give it 0.36% of screen time.

+

The story

A study of 60 globally popular K-dramas (2019–24) found climate content in just 4 of 1,135 viewing hours — mostly recycling scenes, usually resolved in a single episode (Forecasting Love and Weather is a rare exception). Meanwhile the fandom moved first: Kpop4Planet mobilized K-pop fans worldwide to push streaming platforms and brands on climate, and K-pop idols carried climate messages to COP26.

The move

Measure the silence, then fund both sides of it. The gap between audience concern (≈90%) and on-screen presence (0.36%) is a quantified opportunity — and the fandom is already organized infrastructure waiting for partners.

The receipts

Kpop4Planet campaigns have won concrete commitments from major platforms and companies — fan power converted to corporate policy, with the Hallyu wave as the delivery vehicle.

Steal this Don’t wait for the studios. Fund the audit that makes the whitespace undeniable, then back fandom-led campaigns as a parallel narrative channel with global reach.
Music & Live · Global

Coldplay: the tour that made the solution the spectacle

A 59% emissions cut, verified by MIT — powered partly by dancing fans.

+

The story

Coldplay pledged to cut the Music of the Spheres tour’s direct emissions by 50% versus their previous tour — then beat it, hitting 59%, with the numbers verified by MIT’s Environmental Solutions Initiative and no offsets counted. Kinetic dance floors and power bikes let the crowd literally generate the encore; every ticket plants a tree (7M+ planted).

The move

Turn infrastructure into narrative. The audience isn’t told about clean energy — they are the clean energy. The story is embodied, communal, and joyful, then independently verified so it survives scrutiny.

The receipts

The tour’s published, third-party-checked emissions reporting reset expectations for the entire live-events industry and generated years of earned media.

Steal this Fund the verification layer. Independent measurement is what turned a band’s green tour into an industry-shifting proof point — and it’s exactly the kind of unglamorous cost philanthropy is built to cover.
Music & Live · London

Billie Eilish’s Overheated at The O2

Six arena nights in London, converted into a climate summit for fans.

+

The story

In June 2022, during her six-night O2 residency, Billie Eilish — with REVERB, Support+Feed and the venue — staged Overheated: a multi-day climate event mixing activists, designers, and industry leaders, open to the public, with plant-based catering across the arena. The model has since traveled to Atlanta and Berlin.

The move

Convert tour stops into civic moments. The artist acts as convener: tens of thousands of young fans arrive for the music and leave inside a climate community, in their own city.

The receipts

A replicable city-by-city format; Eilish’s broader touring practice (renewables, plant-based, no private flights) earned TIME100 Climate recognition and keeps climate in her global fan conversation.

Steal this Map the next 12 months of major tours through your priority cities and fund the civic wrapper — local activists on the bill, plant-based catering, voter/action booths. The audience is already coming.
Gaming · Global

Playing for the Planet: climate inside 3 billion screens

The biggest entertainment medium on Earth, organized through play itself.

+

The story

The UN-facilitated Playing for the Planet Alliance has enlisted major studios to put climate into gameplay — annual Green Game Jams have reached hundreds of millions of players with in-game forests, restoration mechanics, and real-world tree-planting tie-ins, across titles from mobile puzzles to global franchises.

The move

Stakes in the mechanics, not the cutscene. Games persuade through agency: players protect what they build. A planted in-game forest a player tends beats any loading-screen message.

The receipts

Hundreds of millions of player activations per jam, studio decarbonization commitments, and a standing industry alliance — at audience scale film and TV cannot match.

Steal this Gaming is the most underfunded narrative channel relative to its reach. Fund game-jam prizes, climate design toolkits for studios, and player-action bridges (in-game act → real-world act).
Sport · UK

Forest Green Rovers: the greenest club in the world

A tiny football club that made sustainability its entire identity — and went global.

+

The story

A small Gloucestershire club became the world’s first UN-certified carbon-neutral football club: fully vegan matchday food, an organic pitch, renewable energy, kits made from recycled and plant-based materials. Punching absurdly above its league, it draws fans and media from every continent.

The move

Total identity, not sponsorship. Sustainability isn’t a partnership slide; it’s the club’s story, rivalry banter included. Sport’s tribal loyalty becomes the carrier wave.

The receipts

Global press footprint wildly disproportionate to club size, a worldwide fan community recruited through values, and a template the sports industry now studies.

Steal this In sport, fund the first mover in each league or country — one fully-committed club, team, or athlete reshapes what fans expect from all the others.

05 — In the room

Bring this to London.

A suggested 90-minute working-session arc for funders and partners during Climate Week — designed to leave the room with named plays, not nodding agreement.

  1. 0:00

    Tune in — the whitespace map

    Walk the stats above. Where is climate absent in the media your grantees’ audiences love most? Name three silences.

  2. 0:20

    Case sprint — steal in pairs

    Each pair takes one case file and answers: what would this look like in our portfolio, region, or language? Two minutes to pitch back.

  3. 0:50

    Deal the plays

    Run the play builder live for each funder’s priority audience. Argue with the card. Improve it. The argument is the strategy.

  4. 1:15

    Commit — one tide, one date

    Choose one synchronized moment in the next 12 months (a COP, a climate week, a heat season) and one play each funder will move toward it. Name owners before leaving the room.

Provocations for the pub afterwards

  • If 97% of stories ignore climate, is funding one more documentary the highest-leverage pound we can spend?
  • The UK soap crossover took a producer with an idea and months of backstage diplomacy. Who plays that role in your region — and who pays for it?
  • What is climate philanthropy’s “Just Look Up” — the phrase we want strangers arguing about next year?
